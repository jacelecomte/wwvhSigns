Warren Woods Veterinary Hospital Vaccine Consent Form

Warren Woods Veterinary Hospital is continuing to provide the best recommendations for the optimum health of your pet. Vaccine recommendations are currently being researched by various veterinary health organizations and are in the process of being modified. Currently the American Veterinary Medical Association is recommending core vaccination programs be developed by veterinarians based on the patients lifestyle and risk factors. Core vaccines are considered those that protect them from diseases that are widely distributed in the region, virulent, and highly infectious, thereby posing a risk of severe disease. Core vaccines are efficacious and exhibit patient benefit: risk ratios high enough to warrant their use, and/or are of considerable public health significance, or required by law.

Core Vaccines at Warren Woods Veterinary Hospital include:
Canine: Rabies, Distemper aka DHLPP - Distemper, Hepatitis, Leptospirosis, Parainfluenza, and Parvovirus.
Feline: Rabies, Distemper aka FVRCP - Viral Rhinotracheitis, Calicivirus, and Panleukopenia.

Non-core Vaccines include:
Canine: Kennel Cough (Bordetella), Lyme, Influenza
Feline: Infectious Peritonitis and Leukemia.

Warren Woods Veterinary Hospital recommends vaccination with core vaccines to puppies and kittens every 3-4 weeks until 16 weeks of age then an annual booster vaccination until five years of age. At five years of age vaccination protocols will be modified based on the patient's lifestyle and risk exposure (an exception to this protocol is the rabies vaccine that is governed by city and state regulations). In cases of adverse vaccine reaction by the patient, vaccinations may be stopped and titers may be recommended. Non-core vaccines will be given at the owner's and veterinarian's discretion based on the patient's exposure risk.
Although most pets respond well to vaccines, like any medical procedure, vaccination carries some risk. The most common adverse responses are mild and short-term, including fever, sluggishness and reduced appetite. Pets may also experience temporary pain or subtle swelling at the site of vaccination. Although most adverse responses will resolve within a day or two, excessive pain, swelling or listlessness should be discussed with your veterinarian.

Rarely, serious adverse responses occur. Contact your veterinarian immediately if your pet has repeated vomiting or diarrhea, whole body itching, difficulty breathing, collapse or swelling of the face or legs. These signs may indicate an allergic reaction. In very rare instances, death can occur.
